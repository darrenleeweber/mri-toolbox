Useful links

This atlas uses MRI scans with a few drawings to illustrate the brain. It is also useful to look at diagrams, photographs and cell-staining images of thebrain to get a good feeling for neuroanatomical structures. Here is a short list of e-eptional neuroanatomy atlases.

  • Jody Culham's excellent introduction to neuroanatomy, she also has a nice set of images from the Duvernoy atlas.
  • Washington University provides a nice set of microtome photographs.
  • The Talairach Daemon includes a nice set of diagrams of a normalized brain that can highlight target structures (interactive features require Java).
  • The whole brain atlas includes T1 and T2 images that are great for learning about neuroanatomy.
  • Nicola Petitti includes a nice guide for finding landmarks using CT or MRI scans. Particularly good at describing how to find the central sulcus and surrounding regions.
  • Automatic Anatomic Labelling (AAL) map identifies landmarks on the CH2 image used for my own atlas. Using this map 'yoked' to a with a normalized brain is a good way to get a ballpark idea of which anatomical structures you are looking at. Likewise, the Brodmann map is also based on the ch2 brain.
  • Temple University has a nice set of MRI and CT atlases.
  • The internet pathology laboratory will interactively label regions on photographs of the brain (interactive features require a browser with Javascript).
  • The University of Michigan Medical School has a great set of interactive neuroanatomy tutorials (interactive features require a browser with Javascript).
  • Looking at a plastinated brain is a useful way to learn about sucli and gyri.
  • The Digital Anatomist Project includes a number of 3D surface renderings that can help you understand the 3D shape of complex structures such as the limbic system (fornix and hippocampus). This site also allows you to interactively click on a view of the brain and see the corresponding structure (e.g. the angular gyrus).

Chris Rorden, 13 October 2002
